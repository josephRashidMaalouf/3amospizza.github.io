0 — 200 m

Sunlit Zone

You can still see the surface above you — a trembling silver mirror. Sunlight reaches you here, diffused and dreamy, turning everything into shifting cathedral light. Ninety percent of all ocean life lives in this thin, luminous strip.

Every second breath you take was made by the ocean.
200 — 1,000 m

Twilight Zone

The last traces of sunlight dissolve into a perpetual blue-grey dusk. Here, creatures have evolved their own light — bioluminescence, cold fire born from chemistry alone. They flash, they lure, they signal across distances that would swallow cities.

Eighty percent of the ocean has never been seen by human eyes.
1,000 — 4,000 m

Midnight Zone

No light. No warmth. The pressure here is four hundred times what you feel at the surface — enough to collapse a human body in an instant. And yet life endures. Impossible, patient, alien life that has never once seen the sun.

The anglerfish dangles its own light like a promise in absolute dark.
4,000 — 6,000 m

Abyssal Zone

Almost nothing survives here. The few creatures that do feed on what drifts down from above — the bodies of whales, dead algae, fragments of a world they will never know. They call it “marine snow.” It takes weeks to fall.

This is what patience looks like.
6,000 — 11,000 m

Hadal Zone

The Mariana Trench. The lowest place on Earth. If you dropped Mount Everest here, its peak would still be more than a mile underwater. Tiny crustaceans live at this depth under pressure that would crush a submarine to foil.

More people have walked on the Moon than have been here.

You are the ocean.

The atoms in your body have cycled through every ocean on Earth for four billion years. The iron in your blood was forged inside a star that died before the Sun existed.

You are not a visitor to this universe.

You are how the universe experiences itself.
