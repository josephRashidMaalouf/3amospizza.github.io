Deep sea

INHABITANTS

The creatures that own the dark.

1,000 — 4,000 m

Anglerfish

The female dangles a bioluminescent lure to draw prey into absolute darkness. The male, a fraction of her size, fuses to her body and is absorbed — his eyes dissolve, his organs go. He lives on as a pair of gonads.

0 — 700 m

Crystal Jellyfish

Nearly invisible in the water column. When threatened, it releases a ring of cold blue-green light — the same protein, GFP, that revolutionised biology and won a Nobel Prize. It has no brain, no heart, no blood.

400 — 800 m

Barreleye Fish

Its entire head is a transparent dome of fluid. Inside, two tubular eyes rotate on gimbals — they can look straight up through its own skull to spot the silhouettes of prey against the faint surface light above.

600 — 1,200 m

Vampire Squid

Neither squid nor octopus. It is the sole survivor of an ancient order, unchanged for millions of years. Its red cloak, blue eyes and spiny arms gave it its name. It survives entirely on marine snow — the falling remains of dead life.

200 — 1,500 m

Black Dragonfish

It produces red light from organs beneath its eyes — a frequency invisible to most deep-sea creatures, including its prey. It hunts using a secret wavelength. A private torch in a shared darkness.

3,000 — 7,000 m

Dumbo Octopus

The deepest-living octopus on Earth, named for its ear-like fins. It swallows prey whole and intact. Unlike all other octopuses, it has no ink sac — at these depths there is nothing to hide from. Nothing else lives here.
