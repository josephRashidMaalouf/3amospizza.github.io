01

The ocean makes oxygen

Between 50 and 80 percent of Earth's oxygen is produced by marine phytoplankton — microscopic organisms floating in the sunlit zone. The Amazon is not the lungs of the Earth. The ocean is.

02

Pressure as a measure of depth

At 11,000 metres, the pressure is approximately 1,100 bar — equivalent to 50 jumbo jets stacked on top of you. The Trieste, the first vessel to reach Challenger Deep in 1960, had windows 15 centimetres thick.

03

Marine snow

The deep ocean is fed by a constant rain of organic matter from above — dead plankton, faecal pellets, fragments of creatures. It drifts down for weeks. At the bottom, it accumulates as a soft blanket of sediment kilometres deep.

04

Sound travels further underwater

Sound moves nearly five times faster in water than in air. The SOFAR channel — a layer of water at around 1,000m — acts as a natural waveguide. Blue whales can communicate across entire ocean basins.

05

Cold seeps and hydrothermal vents

On the ocean floor, hydrothermal vents exhale superheated water rich in minerals at 400°C. Entire ecosystems exist here with no sunlight at all — powered entirely by chemosynthesis. Life does not require a star.

06

The ocean absorbs our carbon

The ocean has absorbed roughly 30% of all CO₂ emitted by humans since industrialisation. This is slowing climate change. It is also acidifying the water, dissolving the shells of everything that lives in it.

We have mapped more of the surface of Mars than we have of our own ocean floor.

The most alien world we know of is right here, beneath the surface of this one.
